About the Organization

Heavy Vehicles Factory (HVF), Avadi, is a cornerstone of India's defense manufacturing sector. Established in 1961 and now operating under Armoured Vehicles Nigam Limited (AVNL), HVF is central to producing and maintaining India's fleet of armored combat vehicles.

Major Platforms Manufactured:

HVF is responsible for producing some of the most advanced armored vehicles in India’s defense arsenal:

  • Arjun MBT: India's indigenous third-generation main battle tank.
  • T-90 Bhishma: A license-built adaptation of the modern Russian T-90.
  • T-72 Ajeya: Upgraded variants forming the backbone of India's armored corps.
  • Combat Engineering Vehicles: Including Bridge Layer Tanks (BLTs) and Armoured Recovery Vehicles (ARVs).

The Project: Autonomous JetTank

The core of my internship was a technical project focused on the HiWonder JetTank, a ROS-based robotic platform powered by an NVIDIA Jetson Nano. The goal was to develop and implement key automation features for unmanned ground vehicles (UGVs).

We worked on a suite of algorithms for color tracking, vision-based obstacle avoidance, LiDAR mapping, and waypoint navigation, gaining insight into how sensor data can drive intelligent decision-making on embedded systems.

Developed Algorithms & Systems

1. Vision-Based Path Tracking

Enables the robot to follow a colored path using OpenCV for real-time image processing and a proportional controller for course correction.

2. Vision-Based Obstacle Avoidance

Uses Canny edge detection within specific zones of a camera's Region of Interest (ROI) to identify and navigate around obstacles.

3. Pre-defined Path Navigation via CSV

Allows the robot to follow a precise path defined by (x, y) coordinates from a CSV file, using a PID controller for accurate movement.

4. LiDAR-Based Guarding and Mapping

Implemented LiDAR for various behaviors, including maintaining distance from objects and generating real-time 2D environment maps in RViz.

Design-Focused Study of Hydrodynamic Flow Characteristics in Underwater Vehicles

ANSYS Fluent – CFD Simulation

A simulation project to analyze the external flow over conceptual submarine and submersible models to understand how hull shape affects performance and drag.

Models & Meshing

The conceptual models were created using Autodesk Fusion 360. The study compares a large submarine (approx. 70m x 10m) with the more compact Titan submersible (approx. 17.5m x 5m). A tetrahedral mesh with inflation layers was used to accurately capture flow behavior near the vehicle surfaces.

Conceptual Submarine Model

Titan Submersible Model

Tetrahedral Mesh with Inflation Layers

Comparative Simulation Results

Velocity Contours

The submarine's streamlined shape allows for efficient flow acceleration, while the Titan shows a larger, more turbulent wake region, indicating higher form drag.

Velocity Streamlines

Streamlines around the submarine are more ordered and follow the hull curvature, suggesting higher hydrodynamic efficiency. The Titan shows more chaotic streamlines near the rear, implying less streamlined shaping.

Velocity Vectors

The submarine shows a more elongated and symmetric wake due to its streamlined geometry. The Titan exhibits more abrupt flow detachment and a turbulent wake, characteristic of a bluff body.

Pressure Contours

High-pressure zones are visible at the front stagnation points. The pressure drop along the submarine's hull is more gradual, confirming smoother flow and lower drag.

Submarine Performance

Drag force increases with velocity, while the drag coefficient remains stable and low.

Titan Submersible Performance

Shows significantly higher drag coefficient due to its less streamlined, bluff body shape.

Thermal Conductivity Analysis of Copper

Lab-Based Project using TPS 2500S

A hands-on experimental project to precisely measure the thermal conductivity of copper at various temperatures using a Hot Disk sensor.

Methodology & Equipment

This experiment utilized the Transient Plane Source (TPS) method with a TPS 2500 S Hot Disk instrument. This is a highly precise technique for determining thermal transport properties. A Hot Disk sensor, consisting of a nickel double spiral, acts as both a heat source and a dynamic temperature sensor, allowing for accurate measurements.

TPS 2500S.

Experimental setup with sample holder.

Temperature Sensor.

Experimental Results

Key Findings

The thermal conductivity of a 50x50x5 mm copper sample was measured at three different temperatures. The results showed high precision and repeatability, with mean conductivity values reaching up to 383.76 W/m·K at room temperature.

  • At -10°C, Mean Conductivity: 344.42 W/m·K
  • At 21°C, Mean Conductivity: 383.76 W/m·K
  • At 60°C, Mean Conductivity: 381.45 W/m·K

Aerodynamic Study of a Sports Ball

ANSYS Fluent – Conceptual CFD Analysis

A conceptual study using ANSYS Fluent to visualize and understand the complex airflow patterns and flow separation regions around a stationary golf ball.

Methodology & Setup

This conceptual study involved modeling a standard, dimpled golf ball in CAD software and then importing it into ANSYS Fluent. A fluid domain was created around the ball to simulate airflow. The primary goal was not to gather precise numerical data but to visually understand the fundamental aerodynamic principles at play, such as boundary layer separation and wake formation.

3D model of a dimpled golf ball.

Fluid domain setup for external flow analysis.

Simulation Results & Insights

Flow Visualization

The simulation visually demonstrated how the dimples on the golf ball's surface create a thin, turbulent boundary layer. This energized layer helps the airflow adhere to the ball's surface for longer, delaying flow separation and resulting in a much smaller wake. This reduction in the wake is the primary reason for the significant decrease in pressure drag, allowing the ball to travel much farther than a smooth sphere.
